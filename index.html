Leg 1 · 5 Nights

Seoul — Myeongdong

Myeongdong is the most convenient first base in Seoul — chaotic, bright, touristy, and surrounded by some of the city's best sightseeing, food, and nightlife. The royal palace corridor, historic markets, hidden neighbourhoods, and Bukhansan National Park are all within easy reach. Use these 5 nights to build a rhythm between the big sights and the quieter, more local corners Seoul rewards curious visitors with.

Getting into Seoul
Airport
AREX Express from Incheon
43 minutes direct to Hongdae or Seoul Station. Use T-money card. Buy a pocket WiFi or SIM at the airport. The express train is far faster than a taxi and costs around ₩9,500. Your L7 Hongdae hotel is a 5-min walk from Hongik University Station — exit 9.
Pick up T-money at any convenience store or subway booth. It works on every subway, bus, and even most taxis across all of Korea.
Evening
From 3pm
Myeongdong Night Market Kakao Maps
The street market ignites from around 3pm, with hundreds of vendors lining the walking street and spilling into alleys. Your first-night orientation in Korean food: tteokbokki (spicy rice cakes), hotteok (sweet brown sugar pancake), dakgangjeong (glazed fried chicken bites), odeng (fish cake skewers on sticks with warm broth), and tornado potatoes.
The street right off the main drag — Kalguksu Alley — has excellent hand-cut noodle restaurants for only ₩9,000–11,000. Myeongdong Kyoja (Michelin Bib Gourmand since 2017) is here — expect queues, but a second branch opened near the station exit 8.
Night
Coin Noraebang
The jet-lag cure: a private karaoke room where you pay per song. "Coin noraebangs" charge ₩500–1,000 per song, they never close, and they're one of Korea's most joyful social institutions. Pick English-language songs or attempt a Korean ballad. No judgement. Snacks and soju optional but recommended.
Day 2
Royal Palace + Hanok Villages + Gwangjang Market

Morning — Palaces
Choose your palace (or do both — they're 10 min apart)
Option A — Grand & Iconic
Gyeongbokgung Palace Kakao Maps
Seoul's largest palace (1394). Arrive at 10am for the elaborate Changing of the Guard. Rent a hanbok from nearby shops — wearing one gets you free entry to all royal palaces. The National Folk Museum is free inside the grounds.
Option B — More Beautiful, Less Crowded
Changdeokgung + Secret Garden Kakao Maps
UNESCO-listed, widely considered the "most Korean" palace. The Secret Garden (Huwon) — 78 acres of royal pleasure garden — is only accessible by guided tour (English available). Only 100 visitors per slot. Stunning in any season.
Book Secret Garden tickets well in advance at cdg.go.kr. Tickets: ₩5,000 on top of ₩3,000 palace entry. The ₩6,000 Royal Palace Combo Pass covers all 4 palaces + Jongmyo Shrine for 6 months — great value.
Late Morning
11am–1pm
Seochon + Tongin Market
West of Gyeongbokgung lies Seochon — one of Seoul's oldest and most soulful neighbourhoods. Narrow alleys between 100-year-old hanok homes, local cafés, art studios, and galleries that still feel lived-in rather than tourist-packaged. The Tongin Market yeopjeon dosirak experience is unmissable: pay ₩5,000 at the Dosirak Café, receive old brass coins (yeopjeon), and use them at different stalls to assemble your own lunchbox — tteokbokki, fish cake, dumplings, rice balls. Eat in the upstairs hanok space.
11am–1pm is the perfect window at Tongin Market. The best stalls run out of ingredients by 2pm.
Alt: 11am
Bukchon Hanok Village
North of Changdeokgung, a hillside of preserved Joseon-era homes with tiled rooftops and stone alley views. More photogenic and more famous than Seochon, but busier. Wear comfortable shoes — it's hilly. Combine with nearby Samcheong-dong for cafés and gallery-hopping.
Go weekday mornings to beat crowds. Residents still live here and the district has posted quiet-hour requests.
Afternoon
2–5pm
Gwangjang Market Kakao Maps
Seoul's oldest continuously operating market (est. 1905), famous from Netflix Street Food: Asia. The 2nd floor food alley is the main event. Eat standing or perched at tiny stalls elbow-to-elbow with locals.

What to eat: mayak gimbap (tiny "drug" rice rolls — one roll is never enough), bindaetteok (mung bean pancakes, the market's signature — pair with makgeolli ₩3,000/bowl), yukhoe (fresh beef tartare in sesame oil, served with pear — try the yukhoe + nakji set ₩20,000), japchae, and kalguksu. Budget ₩20,000–30,000 to eat properly.
The grandmother vendors at bindaetteok stall 11 are legendary. Visit weekday 12–2pm for the fullest market-life atmosphere. The market closes on Sundays.
Evening
7pm+
Ikseon-dong for drinks + Korean BBQ dinner
Walk 10 minutes from Gwangjang to this 1920s hanok village where old Korean homes have been converted into makgeolli bars, craft beer spots, and upscale restaurants. Explore the alleys, pick a BBQ restaurant on the famous Gogi Street (BBQ alley), and grill samgyeopsal (pork belly) or galbi (beef short ribs) at the table. Order soju + beer (somaek), wrap the meat in perilla leaves with gochujang, and enjoy.

Bukhansan Hike + N Seoul Tower Sunset

Full Day — Choose Your Level
Bukhansan trails — pick your challenge
Hard (6 hours)
Baegundae Peak (836m)
The summit of Bukhansan. Granite slab scramble at the top with ropes. Unobstructed 360° city views. Take Line 3 → Gupabal, then Bus 704/8772 to trailhead. Start by 8am. 3.4km one way, very steep finale.
Moderate (4–5 hours)
Dobongsan
The dramatic, rugged side of the park — sharp granite peaks and ridge walks. Slightly tougher terrain than Baegundae but fewer tourists. Direct subway to Dobongsan Station Line 1/7. Restaurants at the trailhead serve traditional mountain food after the hike.
Easy (2–3 hours)
Inwangsan + Fortress Wall
The best quick city-view hike in Seoul. Follows the old Seoul Fortress Wall with panoramic views over Gyeongbokgung, Namsan Tower, and the Han River. Cultural sites including a shamanic village (Guksadang). Begins near Gyeongbokgung — accessible and rewarding.
Post-hike
Mountain makgeolli culture at the trailhead
This is a sacred Korean ritual. After any mountain hike, stop at the cluster of restaurants at the trailhead and order pajeon (green onion pancake) and a bowl of cold makgeolli. The combination of crispy savoury pancake and fizzy, slightly tangy rice wine after a big hike is one of the best experiences Korea offers. Hikers from age 6 to 86 do this every weekend.
At Dobongsan trailhead, look for restaurants serving dotori-muk (acorn jelly) and deodeokgui (grilled deodeok root) — mountain foods almost impossible to find elsewhere in Seoul.
Sunset
N Seoul Tower (Namsan Mountain) Kakao Maps
Walk the Namsan circuit trail (50 min easy loop) or take the cable car up from Myeongdong. The tower has an observatory, multiple restaurants, and the famous love-lock fence — add yours like every K-drama protagonist. Best visited 30–60 minutes before sunset when the sky changes and the city lights begin. The tower itself lights up at dark.
The Namsan cable car queues can be long on weekends. Walk up instead — it's only 20–30 minutes along a pleasant forested path.

Seongsu-dong Kakao Maps
Seoul's Brooklyn — former shoe factories turned into the city's most creative neighbourhood. Pop-up stores (new ones appear weekly), concept cafés, independent boutiques, photography studios, and the city's best specialty coffee. Afternoon
2–6pm
Gangnam + Bongeunsa Temple + COEX Starfield
Take Line 2 to Gangnam. Visit Bongeunsa Temple — a 1,200-year-old Buddhist temple impossibly surrounded by glass skyscrapers. Completely free, surprisingly peaceful. Then explore COEX Mall, home to the spectacular Starfield Library — a cathedral of books with floor-to-ceiling shelves that's as much an art installation as a library. Browse, photograph, and sit in one of the reading chairs under the towers of books.
Evening — Korean Drinking Culture

Makgeolli + Sool Bars
Sanullim 1992 (Hongdae) — jumak-style traditional tavern, extensive makgeolli list, incredible spicy octopus somyeon. Walk-in only, expect a wait.

Craft Beer Seoul
Euljiro "Hipjiro" Crawl
One of the most atmospheric bar zones in Seoul. Hidden in former print-shop alleys: Euljiro Brewing (loud, neon, great IPAs + sourdough pizza), Artmonster (retro Hong Kong cinema aesthetic, self-pour wristband system), and the Nogari Fish Dried Alley for pojangmacha vibes with beer.

Korean BBQ + Pojangmacha
Head to Mapo-gu (near Hongdae) for the best local BBQ streets. Start with galbi or samgyeopsal. Move to a pojangmacha tent for fried snacks and soju. End at a chimaek (fried chicken + beer) restaurant for one final round — BHC Chicken near Myeongdong is legendary.

Full Day — Book Ahead
DMZ + JSA Tour
The Korean Demilitarized Zone is one of the most unique experiences in the world. Half-day or full-day guided tours from central Seoul visit the Joint Security Area (Panmunjom), the 3rd Infiltration Tunnel (dug by North Korea), Dora Observatory (overlooking North Korea), and Dorasan Station (the last train stop south of the border). Deeply sobering and fascinating — the most militarised border on earth.
Book through Viator, KKday or a local operator. Most tours depart 8am from Gwanghwamun or Myeongdong. A passport is required. Book at least 48 hours in advance.
Relaxed Seoul Day
Markets + Neighbourhoods
Morning: Cheonggyecheon Stream walk (urban stream beneath the city — the walk between market areas is lovely).
Afternoon: Dongdaemun Design Plaza (DDP) — the futuristic building by Zaha Hadid hosts design exhibitions and surrounds a moat-like plaza.
Late PM: Myeongdong K-beauty haul — Olive Young Global (12-floor), 12-storey Daiso, HBAF almond stores, duty-free shopping at Lotte.
Evening
Hangang River Picnic + Sunset
End the Seoul leg the local way: buy fried chicken and a bottle of Cass beer from a convenience store, rent a bike from one of the stations on the riverbank, and cycle along the Han River to find a grassy spot by the water. This is what every Seoulite does in good weather. The Yeouido stretch has great facilities and views of the bridges.
The Rainbow Bridge (Banpo Bridge) has a fountain light show from its spans at night. Good viewing spot is just east of the bridge.

Leg 2 · 4 Nights

Busan

Korea's coastal soul — a city where mountains collapse into the sea, where refugees built rainbow villages on cliff sides, where the largest fish market in Korea operates 24 hours, and where the cold noodles taste different from anywhere else in the country. Busan balances city energy with genuine coastal calm. Stay near Gwangalli or Haeundae beach for the best access — the Busan subway connects the main areas easily.

Travel
Seoul → Busan by KTX
The KTX high-speed train is the best way to travel. 2 hours 15 minutes from Seoul Station to Busan Station. Book at least a few days ahead at Korail.go.kr or on the Korail app. Trains run frequently and the ride is comfortable. A luxury express bus (4 hours) also runs if you want to save money — has lie-flat seats.

From Nine Tree Myeongdong: Take Line 4 from Myeongdong Station → Hoehyeon → change to Line 1 → Seoul Station (about 15 min total). Your Lotte Hotel Busan is 3 stops on Line 1 from Busan Station → Seomyeon (about 8 min).
Afternoon
Gamcheon Culture Village Kakao Maps
Busan's most iconic sight — a Korean War refugee camp that transformed into an extraordinary hillside art district, with narrow alleys, pastel houses, wall murals, tiny galleries, and indie cafés cascading down the hillside to the sea. Wander freely — you don't need a map, getting lost is the point. The Avant Garde Café at the top has the best elevated panorama over the whole village.
Go on a weekday afternoon (3–5pm) when tour groups thin out. The village becomes genuinely atmospheric at dusk when residents come home and the alleys quiet down.
Evening
Gwangalli Beach + Drone Light Show Kakao Maps
Busan's coolest beach — local, laid-back, with spectacular views of the illuminated Gwangan Bridge. The promenade has brilliant cafés and seafood restaurants with direct beach views. Every Saturday evening there's a free synchronized drone light show over the water. Walk the full beach and pick a spot for dinner.

Gwangalli seafood restaurants — fresh hoe (raw fish) with Gwangan Bridge views
Day 2
Sea Temples + Haeundae + Blueline Park
culturenatureactive
▼
7–9am
Haedong Yonggungsa Temple Kakao Maps
One of the most extraordinary temples in Korea — built in 1376 directly on coastal cliffs above the crashing East Sea. Stone lanterns, Buddha statues, and pagodas on the rock platforms with waves breaking below. Magnificent at low tide when you can walk the shoreline around the base. Visit before 9am to avoid the tour buses completely.
Nearby eateries serve simple vegetarian bibimbap after the temple visit — perfect morning meal with ocean views.
Mid-morning
Seokbulsa or Beomeosa Temple
Seokbulsa — hidden cliff temple with giant Buddhas carved directly into the rock face. Almost no tourists even know this exists. The approach through a narrow valley past waterfalls is extraordinary.

Beomeosa — a sprawling 1,300-year-old mountain temple in pine forest. You can hike from here up to Geumjeongsan Fortress for panoramic city views. Temple stay programs available for overnight cultural immersion.
Seokbulsa: take Subway Line 1 to Beomeo Station, then taxi. Beomeosa: take Line 1 to Beomneosa Station and walk 30 mins or take Bus 90.
Afternoon
Haeundae Beach + Blueline Sky Capsule Kakao Maps
Korea's most famous beach — 1.5km of white sand with facilities, restaurants, and a promenade. Ride the Haeundae Blueline Park Sky Capsule — colourful glass pods on a coastal rail track running from Mipo to Songjeong with breathtaking ocean views. The ride takes about 30 minutes each way. Also worth: walking around Dongbaekseom Island's coastal trail (free, peaceful, with sea views).
Book Sky Capsule tickets online 1–2 weeks ahead — they sell out, especially weekends.
Evening
Bay 101 + Night Views
Bay 101 yacht club at Haeundae has free entry and one of the best views of Busan's lit-up skyline at night. Browse the marina, get a drink at the bar, and watch the city reflect in the water. Dinner at Nasari Restaurant near Gwangalli for ocean views with Korean food.
Busan Day 2 Food
Dwaeji gukbap — pork rice soup, Busan's essential breakfast. Try Geukdong Dwaeji Gukbap near Haeundae
Milmyeon — cold wheat noodles, Busan's answer to naengmyeon. Try Haeundae Gaya Milmyeon
Ssiat hotteok — seed-filled sweet pancake at BIFF Square, the best street dessert in Busan

Morning
Jagalchi Fish Market + Nampo-dong Kakao Maps
Korea's largest seafood market, run almost entirely by women called "Jagalchi ajumma." A riot of colour, noise, and overwhelming fresh sea smell — live octopus, giant king crabs, haenyeo-caught abalone, sea cucumber. Buy fresh seafood on the ground floor and take it to the restaurants upstairs to have it prepared.

Walk to nearby Nampo-dong for BIFF Square (film festival hub with great street food), Bosu Book Street (vintage books, stationery, quiet atmosphere), and Bupyeong Kkangtong Market (night market come alive after 7pm).
Get the yukhoe (raw beef tartare) and hoe (fresh sliced fish) combination. Budget ₩30,000–50,000 for a proper seafood spread upstairs with soju.
Afternoon
Choose your coast:
Dramatic Cliffs
Taejongdae Resort Park
The southern tip of Yeongdo Island — dramatic coastal bluffs, lighthouse views, crashing waves, hidden temples, and a small zipline. Walk the coastal trail (90 min loop) or take the Danubi miniature forest train if you'd rather not hike. Genuinely spectacular.
Skywalk + Cable Car
Songdo Beach
The Songdo Cloud Trail is a swirling glass-floored walkway over the sea. The Busan Air Cruise (Songdo Cable Car, now with glass-bottom cars) crosses the bay to Amnam Park. Less crowded than Haeundae. Excellent for photos.
Hidden Coastal Village
Huinnyeoul Culture Village + Jeoryeong Trail
Often called the "Santorini of Busan" for its blue-and-white painted buildings. Walk the Jeoryeong Coastal Walk (part of the 35km trail) for sea cliff views. Find the colourful piano stairs. Very few tourists compared to Gamcheon.
Evening
Seomyeon for food and nightlife
Busan's most local dining and drinking zone — university students, office workers, and locals who haven't yet moved to Seoul. Food alley near Seomyeon Station has brilliant dakgalbi (spicy stir-fried chicken with stretchy mozzarella), pojangmacha tents, and late-night tteokbokki. Great for cheap street food followed by a wander through the underground shopping arcade.

Ahopsan Forest + Mountain Hike + Heosimcheong Spa
natureactive

Morning
Ahopsan Forest (Bamboo Forest) Kakao Maps
A breathtaking bamboo grove in the mountains west of Busan, famously featured in historical K-dramas. Walking through the tall bamboo columns is hushed, green, and otherworldly — an almost Japanese aesthetic in the middle of Korea. Free entry. Best in morning light when shafts filter through.
Mid-morning
Geumjeongsan Fortress Hike
A well-maintained forested mountain trail leading up to ancient stone fortress walls and sweeping views over Busan and the sea. You can take a cable car (₩9,000 return) part-way and hike from there. One of the best city-to-mountain views in Korea. Very popular with Korean hikers — expect grandmas overtaking you.
From the fortress walls, look for the famous Geumjeong Mountain Fortress North Gate (Bukhammun) — it's almost completely intact and makes for dramatic photos.
Afternoon
Heosimcheong Spa (Spa Land) Kakao Maps
Busan's legendary jjimjilbang (Korean spa). Multiple floors of natural hot spring pools, themed sauna rooms (charcoal, Himalayan salt, infrared, jade), scrub treatment rooms, sleeping areas, and a food court serving kimchi jjigae and sikhye. Entry includes towel, locker, and spa clothes. Widely considered one of the best in Korea. Sink in, and don't leave for several hours.
The Italian salt sauna room is a highlight — walls entirely covered in pink Himalayan salt crystals. The open-air rooftop pool (if weather allows) with city views is another standout.

Leg 3 · 4 Nights

Jeju Island — Seogwipo

Jeju is a different country within Korea — a volcanic island with a distinct dialect, food culture, mythology, and landscape. The south coast around Seogwipo is where the best of Jeju concentrates: waterfalls, coastal walks, the best beaches, and the most dramatic natural sights. Rent a car at the airport — Jeju's attractions are spread across the island and buses, while they exist, are too slow and infrequent for efficient exploration. A rental car transforms the trip.

Day 1
Arrival + South Coast + Jeju Black Pork BBQ

Travel
Busan → Jeju domestic flight
~55 minutes from Gimhae Airport (PUS) to Jeju International (CJU). Book with Jeju Air, Air Busan, or Korean Air. From Lotte Hotel Busan: Seomyeon Station Line 2 → Sasang Station → Airport Limousine Bus (or take a KakaoTaxi). Collect rental car at the airport immediately — this is your vehicle for the whole Jeju stay. Head south on Route 1100 towards Seogwipo and Parnas Hotel Jeju.
Afternoon
Jusangjeolli Lava Cliffs Kakao Maps
One of Jeju's most dramatic natural sights: perfect hexagonal basalt columns formed by Hallasan's lava meeting the sea — like the Giant's Causeway, but Korean. Right on the south coast. The viewing boardwalk takes 20–30 minutes and the light is excellent in afternoon. Free.
Late Afternoon
Cheonjiyeon Waterfall + Oedolgae Rock
Cheonjiyeon Waterfall — three-tiered waterfall cascading through subtropical forest into a turquoise pool. The boardwalk approach is beautiful. Said to have healing properties.

Oedolgae Rock — a dramatic 20-metre volcanic monolith rising alone from the sea, visible from a clifftop viewpoint with a short coastal trail. Featured in multiple K-dramas.
Evening
Jeju Black Pork BBQ
Black pigs (heukdwaeji) are raised on a diet that includes barley and fermented food, giving the pork a richer, more complex flavour than mainland pork. The restaurants here wrap your jacket in a plastic bag before the grilling starts — so it doesn't smell of smoke. This is the definitive Jeju meal. Pork belly and neck cuts at the table, grilled by the staff, wrapped in perilla leaves.
Best with Jeju hallabong soju (citrus-flavoured, lighter than mainland) or local Hallasan makgeolli. The black pork street near Jeju City airport area has dozens of competing restaurants — in Seogwipo, try Heukdwaeji Sikdang.
Day 2
Hallasan National Park — Korea's Highest Summit
natureactive
▼
Choose your Hallasan experience
Full Summit — all day
Seongpanak Trail (summit)
9.6km one way, 7–9 hours round trip. Through volcanic landscapes, ancient forest, and open crater views to the summit lake. The most popular summit trail. Start absolutely by 7am — there is a strict cutoff time at the summit. Reserve permits in advance at visithalla.jeju.go.kr.
Moderate — 5–6 hours
Gwaneumsa Trail (crater views)
The most dramatic trail on Hallasan — passes through deep valleys, ancient forest, and opens up to the crater rim. Recommended as a descent route if you summit via Seongpanak. Tougher terrain but more beautiful scenery.
Easy — 2–3 hours, no summit
1100 Altitude Road Drive
Drive the highest road in Korea (1,100m elevation) which passes right below Hallasan's crater. Multiple rest stops, wetland boardwalks, and viewpoints with no hiking required. Follow with the Seogwipo Natural Recreational Forest for an easy 1-hour walk through conifer forest.
Evening
Seogwipo Maeil Olle Market + Recovery dinner
Jeju's best local market — abalone, tangerines, fresh seafood, black pork, and island specialties. After a Hallasan day, order abalone porridge (jeonbok juk): creamy, green, oceanic, and deeply restorative. The market is small enough to do in 30 minutes but its food quality is excellent.
Reserve Hallasan summit permits online before your trip — summit slots are capped and fill weeks ahead in spring and autumn.
Day 3
East Coast — Sunrise Peak + Seopjikoji + Udo Island Option
naturecultureactive
▼
Early AM (5:30am)
Seongsan Ilchulbong — Sunrise Peak Kakao Maps
A UNESCO World Heritage tuff cone formed by a single volcanic eruption. The 30-minute hike to the crater rim is easy and family-friendly. At the top: a panoramic view of the crater bowl, ocean, and Udo Island. In the morning, the light is extraordinary. At the base after descending, watch the haenyeo (female free divers) bring up their catch from the sea — one of the most distinctive cultural experiences in all of Korea.
Arrive by 7am to beat tour groups. The haenyeo demonstrations usually happen between 9–11am at the base near the water.
Mid-morning — choose your east coast activity
Island Day Trip
Udo Island by Electric Bike
A tiny island off the eastern tip of Jeju — take the 15-minute ferry from Seongsan Port and rent an electric bike to circle the island (1 hour loop). Peanut ice cream, blue-green beaches, haenyeo diving offshore, cliffside lighthouse, a single mountain peak with views of Seongsan. One of the most peaceful half-days in Korea.
Coastal Walk
Seopjikoji Cape
A dramatic wind-swept headland with pampas grass fields, volcanic rock, and sea views. Featured in Korean films and GD's famous café. Easy 1-hour circuit trail to the lighthouse tip. Several cliffside cafés with extraordinary views. Peaceful even when crowds are elsewhere.
Olle Trail Walk
Jeju Olle Route 1
A section of the 437km walking trail that circles all of Jeju. Route 1 runs along the east coast through fishing villages, old stone walls, barley fields, and coastal paths. The "ganse" horse mascot at trail posts reminds you to slow down. Download the Naver Hiking map beforehand.
Afternoon
Jeongbang Waterfall
One of Asia's only waterfalls that falls directly into the ocean — 23 metres of water cascading off basalt cliffs straight into the sea. A short clifftop path leads to the viewing platform where you feel the spray. Very dramatic and relatively uncrowded in the afternoon.
Evening
Lee Jung Seop Street, Seogwipo
Named after a beloved Korean artist who lived in Seogwipo, this lively street has artisan shops, galleries, bars, and cafés. Good for an evening wander with local craft beer, followed by a bowl of haemul jeongol (spicy seafood hotpot) for dinner.

Seoul — Hongdae

Seoul's second act, from a completely different base. Hongdae is Seoul at its most electric — university students, street performers, indie musicians, photographers, and a nightlife scene that doesn't stop. Complete Korean Food & Drink Guide ▼
Bindaetteok — Mung Bean Pancake
Where: Gwangjang Market, Seoul
Ground mung beans mixed with pork, bean sprouts, and kimchi, pan-fried until golden. Crispy outside, moist inside. The single best thing to eat at Gwangjang. Pair with makgeolli — this is the classic Korean combination. ₩5,000/pancake.
Mayak Gimbap — "Drug" Rice Rolls
Where: Gwangjang Market (Original Mayak Gimbap stall)
Half the size of regular kimbap, packed with sesame oil, seeds, and pickled radish. The name means "drug" — because you genuinely cannot stop eating them. Queue is always long but moves fast. Order at least 2 rolls. ₩3,000/roll.
Yukhoe — Korean Beef Tartare
Where: Gwangjang Market 2nd floor yukhoe alley
Fresh Hanwoo beef julienned by hand and tossed with sesame oil, pear, garlic, and egg yolk. The quality at Gwangjang is extraordinarily high — vendors source fresh beef every morning. Try the yukhoe + nakji (octopus) set (₩20,000). Pairs beautifully with makgeolli.
Samgyeopsal — Pork Belly BBQ
Where: Any Korean BBQ restaurant
Thick-cut pork belly grilled at the table. Wrap in perilla leaf or lettuce with sliced garlic, gochujang, and kimchi. The act of wrapping (ssam) is social and interactive. Order soju bombs (somaek) alongside. Budget ₩15,000–20,000/person for a full spread.
Galbi — Marinated Short Rib BBQ
Where: Upscale Korean BBQ restaurants
Beef short ribs marinated overnight in soy, garlic, pear, and sesame. More premium and refined than samgyeopsal. The caramelisation on the grill is extraordinary. Always served with banchan (unlimited side dishes). Try bone-in galbi for the real experience.
Dwaeji Gukbap — Pork Rice Soup
Where: Busan (Seomyeon, Haeundae area restaurants)
Milky pork bone broth simmered for hours, with sliced boiled pork and rice. Season with salted shrimp, chives, and gochujang from the condiment pots on the table. Busan's unofficial breakfast and hangover cure. ₩8,000–10,000/bowl.
Milmyeon — Cold Wheat Noodles
Where: Busan (Seomyeon, Choryang Milmyeon Street)
Busan's signature dish — chewy wheat noodles in cold tangy broth (mul milmyeon) or spicy sauce (bibim milmyeon). Made from flour provided by the US Army to Korean War refugees — genuinely historical food. Refreshing even in winter. ₩9,000–10,000/bowl.
Eomuk — Fish Cake Skewers
Where: Street stalls everywhere in Busan, Busan Station area
Busan is the fish cake capital of Korea. Spongy, savoury fish cake on skewers in a light broth. The broth (mul-tteok) is served free in cups on the side — drink it. Cheap (₩1,000/skewer) and comforting. Samjin Eomuk Cafe near Nampo-dong does an elevated version.
Heukdwaeji — Jeju Black Pork BBQ
Where: Jeju Island — Seogwipo and Jeju City black pork streets
Jeju's black pigs produce richer, more complex pork than mainland breeds. The flavour difference is real — richer marbling, deeper taste, chewier texture. Always grilled at the table, always with a plastic bag for your jacket, always with Jeju soju and perilla leaves. ₩15,000–20,000/person.
Jeonbok Juk — Abalone Porridge
Where: Jeju — Seogwipo Maeil Olle Market, coastal restaurants
Green-tinged rice porridge made with abalone and its digestive tract (which gives the green colour). Deeply oceanic, creamy, and intensely savoury. One of the most uniquely Jeju foods. Best as a morning meal after a hike or as recovery food after the beach.
Pajeon + Makgeolli
Where: Traditional restaurants, pojangmacha, mountain trailheads
Savoury green onion pancake (pajeon) with a cold bowl of makgeolli (milky fizzy rice wine). On a rainy day, this pairing is one of Korea's most beloved cultural rituals — even the name for rainy weather in Korean is associated with pajeon cravings. Absolutely do this at a mountain trailhead after hiking.
Chimaek — Fried Chicken + Beer
Where: Everywhere, BHC near Myeongdong especially celebrated
Korea's most beloved casual food culture. Double-fried Korean fried chicken (crispy, lighter batter than Western) served with beer. Eaten late at night while watching sports, after drinking, or just because. Order half soy-garlic, half spicy. BHC and Kyochon are the dominant national chains.

Korean Drinking Culture — The Full Guide ▼
Soju
Korea's national spirit — distilled rice wine, 16–25% ABV. Original soju (Jinro, Chamisul) has a sharp, clean taste. Fruit sojus (grapefruit, strawberry, peach) are sweeter and very popular with tourists. Served cold in small shot glasses. Always pour for others, not yourself.
Makgeolli
Unfiltered, milky, fizzy rice wine. 5–8% ABV. Slightly sweet and tangy, with a lightly fizzy quality. Traditionally served in a large bowl or kettle. The ideal pairing is pajeon (pancake) — especially on a rainy day. Best tried at Gwangjang Market or a traditional makgeolli bar.
Somaek
The beloved Korean "bomb" — pour a shot of soju into half a glass of beer. Classic ratio is 3:7 (soju to beer). Stir by spinning the shot glass with two chopsticks. This is how most Koreans actually drink at dinner.
Anju
Drinking food — essential in Korean drinking culture. You always eat while drinking. Anju can be anything from dried squid and peanuts to pajeon, tteokbokki, or fried chicken. Ordering food with alcohol is expected at pojangmacha and bars.
Pouring etiquette
Pour drinks for everyone at the table before filling your own. Use two hands (or right hand supported at the wrist) when pouring and receiving. Say "geonbae!" for cheers. Never let someone's glass stay empty.
Pojangmacha
Canvas tent pubs — fold-up tables on the street or in covered alley spaces, serving cheap soju, beer, makgeolli, and anju. An institution. Best experienced late at night when the city is winding down. Find them near universities and entertainment districts.
Craft Beer
Best spots: Seongsu (Amazing Brewing Co., Seoul Brewery), Euljiro (Euljiro Brewing, Artmonster), Itaewon (Magpie, The Booth), Hongdae (Magpie Brewing branch, Seoul Brewery Hapjeong). Korean craft beer has become genuinely excellent — IPAs, sours, and experimental brews.
Jjimjilbang
Korean communal sauna — a genuine cultural experience, not just a spa. Communal areas (pyjamas provided) include themed sauna rooms, sleeping areas, and food courts. Women's/men's bathing areas are separate and clothing-free. Dragon Hill Spa (Yongsan) and Heosimcheong (Busan) are the best. ₩10,000–15,000 all-in.
Cultural Etiquette ▼
Bowing
A slight bow (15°) is the standard greeting. Deep bows are for formal occasions. Nodding while saying annyeonghaseyo (안녕하세요) is perfectly appropriate for most interactions.
Two hands
Use two hands (or right hand + left supporting the wrist) when giving or receiving anything: money, cards, gifts, food, business cards. This shows respect, especially to elders.
Shoes
Remove shoes when entering homes and some traditional restaurants. A shoe rack at the entrance is the signal. Never step on the raised floor area with shoes on.
Eating together
Korean meals are communal — dishes are shared from the centre. Never stick chopsticks vertically into rice (funeral association). Elders eat first. Don't refuse food that's been offered.
Photography
Always ask before photographing individuals, especially ajummas (older women) at markets. Koreans have constitutionally protected portrait rights — photographing someone without consent can be legally problematic.
Useful phrases
Annyeonghaseyo — hello | Gamsahamnida — thank you | Juseyo — please give me [this] | Eolmayeyo? — how much? | Geonbae! — cheers! | Jugeunde? — is [this] available? Koreans respond warmly to any attempt.
