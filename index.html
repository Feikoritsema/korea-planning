Seoul — Myeongdong

Myeongdong is the most convenient first base in Seoul — chaotic, bright, touristy, and surrounded by some of the city's best sightseeing, food, and nightlife. The royal palace corridor, historic markets, hidden neighbourhoods, and Bukhansan National Park are all within easy reach. Use these 5 nights to build a rhythm between the big sights and the quieter, more local corners Seoul rewards curious visitors with.

Morning — Palaces

Option A — Grand & Iconic
Gyeongbokgung Palace Kakao Maps
Seoul's largest palace (1394). Arrive at 10am for the elaborate Changing of the Guard. Rent a hanbok from nearby shops — wearing one gets you free entry to all royal palaces. The National Folk Museum is free inside the grounds.
Option B — More Beautiful, Less Crowded
Changdeokgung + Secret Garden Kakao Maps
UNESCO-listed, widely considered the "most Korean" palace. The Secret Garden (Huwon) — 78 acres of royal pleasure garden — is only accessible by guided tour (English available). Only 100 visitors per slot. Stunning in any season.
Book Secret Garden tickets well in advance at cdg.go.kr. Tickets: ₩5,000 on top of ₩3,000 palace entry. The ₩6,000 Royal Palace Combo Pass covers all 4 palaces + Jongmyo Shrine for 6 months — great value.
Late Morning
11am–1pm
Seochon + Tongin Market
West of Gyeongbokgung lies Seochon — one of Seoul's oldest and most soulful neighbourhoods. Narrow alleys between 100-year-old hanok homes, local cafés, art studios, and galleries that still feel lived-in rather than tourist-packaged. The Tongin Market yeopjeon dosirak experience is unmissable: pay ₩5,000 at the Dosirak Café, receive old brass coins (yeopjeon), and use them at different stalls to assemble your own lunchbox — tteokbokki, fish cake, dumplings, rice balls. Eat in the upstairs hanok space.
11am–1pm is the perfect window at Tongin Market. The best stalls run out of ingredients by 2pm.
Alt: 11am
Bukchon Hanok Village
North of Changdeokgung, a hillside of preserved Joseon-era homes with tiled rooftops and stone alley views. More photogenic and more famous than Seochon, but busier. Wear comfortable shoes — it's hilly. Combine with nearby Samcheong-dong for cafés and gallery-hopping.
Go weekday mornings to beat crowds. Residents still live here and the district has posted quiet-hour requests.
Afternoon
2–5pm
Gwangjang Market Kakao Maps
Seoul's oldest continuously operating market (est. 1905), famous from Netflix Street Food: Asia. The 2nd floor food alley is the main event. Eat standing or perched at tiny stalls elbow-to-elbow with locals.

What to eat: mayak gimbap (tiny "drug" rice rolls — one roll is never enough), bindaetteok (mung bean pancakes, the market's signature — pair with makgeolli ₩3,000/bowl), yukhoe (fresh beef tartare in sesame oil, served with pear — try the yukhoe + nakji set ₩20,000), japchae, and kalguksu. Budget ₩20,000–30,000 to eat properly.
The grandmother vendors at bindaetteok stall 11 are legendary. Visit weekday 12–2pm for the fullest market-life atmosphere. The market closes on Sundays.
Evening
7pm+
Ikseon-dong for drinks + Korean BBQ dinner
Walk 10 minutes from Gwangjang to this 1920s hanok village where old Korean homes have been converted into makgeolli bars, craft beer spots, and upscale restaurants. Explore the alleys, pick a BBQ restaurant on the famous Gogi Street (BBQ alley), and grill samgyeopsal (pork belly) or galbi (beef short ribs) at the table. Order soju + beer (somaek), wrap the meat in perilla leaves with gochujang, and enjoy.

Full Day
DMZ + Joint Security Area (JSA)
The Korean Demilitarized Zone is one of the most extraordinary places on earth — the most heavily militarised border in the world, frozen in a 70-year ceasefire. Your guided tour visits the Joint Security Area (Panmunjom) where North and South Korean soldiers stand face-to-face, the 3rd Infiltration Tunnel (dug by North Korea and only discovered in 1978), the Dora Observatory with views into North Korea, and Dorasan Station — the last train stop before the border. Deeply sobering, historically compelling, and completely unlike anything else in travel.
Most tours depart 7:30–8am from Gwanghwamun or Myeongdong — check your tour's exact departure point. Bring your passport (it is required by the UN Command for JSA entry). No shorts or open-toed shoes at JSA. The full tour typically returns by 5–6pm.
Evening
Rest + Korean comfort food dinner
The DMZ day is intellectually heavy. Keep the evening simple: walk Cheonggyecheon Stream (underground urban stream, lit up at night), or head back to Myeongdong for Korean BBQ and soju. A good night to try sundubu jjigae (soft tofu stew) or doenjang jjigae (fermented soybean paste stew) somewhere nearby — warming and restorative.

Gyeongju Day Trip — "Korea's Museum Without Walls"
culturenaturefood
▼
Context
Why Gyeongju?
Gyeongju was the capital of the Silla Kingdom for nearly a thousand years (57 BC–935 AD) — at its peak around 750 AD it was the fourth-largest city in the world. Today it's a UNESCO World Heritage city dotted with enormous royal burial mounds, ancient temples, a granite Buddha grotto, and a trendy café street that has somehow grown up around 1,300-year-old ruins. A day here is a complete tonal shift from Busan's coastal energy — slower, older, extraordinarily rich in history. Often called "the city without walls" because the whole place is an open-air museum.
Getting There
Busan → Gyeongju: Two Options
Option A — KTX (fastest): Take KTX from Busan Station to Gyeongju Station — just 2 stops, around 30–40 minutes. Book at Korail.go.kr. From Gyeongju Station, buses and taxis reach the town centre in 15–20 minutes.

Option B — Express Bus (easier for the city centre): Take Busan Subway Line 1 to Nopo Station, then the express bus to Gyeongju Bus Terminal — about 1 hour, buses run every 15 minutes. The bus terminal drops you very close to Hwangnidan-gil and the main sights — no transfer needed. Cheapest and often most convenient.

Option C — Guided tour: Multiple operators run full-day guided tours from Busan with pick-up at Seomyeon, Haeundae, or Busan Station. Convenient if you want context and transport sorted. Book via GetYourGuide or Klook.
The bus from Nopo Terminal is the sweet spot — cheap, frequent, and it deposits you right in the historic centre.
Morning
Bulguksa Temple + Seokguram Grotto Bulguksa
Korea's most celebrated Buddhist temple complex — a UNESCO World Heritage Site built in 528 AD during the height of the Silla Kingdom. Set in forested hillside, it houses two iconic stone pagodas (Dabotap and Seokgatap) and intricate wooden halls. Allow 1.5–2 hours to explore properly.

From Bulguksa, the 15-minute drive uphill brings you to Seokguram Grotto — a granite cave sheltering an extraordinary seated Buddha carved in the 8th century, considered the finest example of Buddhist art in East Asia. The cliff-top walk has panoramic views of the sea on a clear day.
Local Bus 10 or 11 connects Gyeongju Bus Terminal → Bulguksa → Seokguram (runs every 20–30 min). Or take a taxi.
Lunch
Gyeongju Local Food
Ssambap — rice wrapped in leafy greens with doenjang (fermented soybean paste) and various side dishes. Very Gyeongju. Hwangnam bread (황남빵) — the city's most famous snack: small red bean paste pastries baked since 1939. Buy a box near the tomb complex. Gyeongju beopju — a 350-year-old traditional rice wine still produced by the same family using a Joseon Dynasty recipe. One of Korea's finest traditional liquors.
Afternoon
Daereungwon Royal Tomb Complex + Hwangnidan-gil Street Daereungwon
Daereungwon is a park of enormous grassy burial mounds — tumuli of the ancient Silla kings and queens. You can enter one tomb (Cheonmachong) to see the burial chamber and the famous "flying horse" painting inside. The mounds are extraordinary to walk among, covered in grass, scattered across a park the size of several city blocks.

Walk 5 minutes to Hwangnidan-gil — Gyeongju's "hip" street with cafés and boutiques in old Korean houses, lined with beautiful views of the tombs. One of those places that makes you realise Korea's past and present are inseparable.
Late Afternoon / Dusk
Donggung Palace & Wolji Pond (Anapji) Kakao Maps
A former royal banquet hall complex (674 AD) reconstructed around an artificial pond. Beautiful in any light, but transformative at sunset when the buildings and lanterns reflect across the still water. Stay for the evening when the whole site is illuminated. Easily the best time to visit is 1 hour before sunset. This is the natural endpoint to a Gyeongju day before heading back to Busan.
Tours from Busan typically end here at dusk and return by 9–10pm. If going independently, last buses from Gyeongju to Busan Nopo Terminal run until around 9pm — check the schedule.
Also Consider
Cheomseongdae Observatory + Gyochon Hanok Village + Woljeonggyo Bridge
Cheomseongdae — East Asia's oldest surviving astronomical observatory (632 AD), a graceful stone tower. A 5-minute walk from Daereungwon.

Gyochon Hanok Village — a preserved village of traditional stone-walled Joseon-era homes. The Gyodong Beopju distillery here still produces the 350-year-old beopju rice wine using the original recipe.

Woljeonggyo Bridge — a reconstructed Silla-era bridge, photogenic and especially beautiful illuminated at night.

7–9am
Haedong Yonggungsa Temple Kakao Maps
One of the most extraordinary temples in Korea — built in 1376 directly on coastal cliffs above the crashing East Sea. Stone lanterns, Buddha statues, and pagodas on the rock platforms with waves breaking below. Magnificent at low tide when you can walk the shoreline around the base. Visit before 9am to avoid the tour buses completely.
Nearby eateries serve simple vegetarian bibimbap after the temple visit — perfect morning meal with ocean views.
Mid-morning
Seokbulsa or Beomeosa Temple
Seokbulsa — hidden cliff temple with giant Buddhas carved directly into the rock face. Almost no tourists even know this exists. The approach through a narrow valley past waterfalls is extraordinary.

Beomeosa — a sprawling 1,300-year-old mountain temple in pine forest. You can hike from here up to Geumjeongsan Fortress for panoramic city views. Temple stay programs available for overnight cultural immersion.
Seokbulsa: take Subway Line 1 to Beomeo Station, then taxi. Beomeosa: take Line 1 to Beomneosa Station and walk 30 mins or take Bus 90.

Leg 3 · 4 Nights

Jeju Island — Seogwipo

Jeju is a different country within Korea — a volcanic island with a distinct dialect, food culture, mythology, and landscape. The south coast around Seogwipo is where the best of Jeju concentrates: waterfalls, coastal walks, the best beaches, and the most dramatic natural sights. Rent a car at the airport — Jeju's attractions are spread across the island and buses, while they exist, are too slow and infrequent for efficient exploration. A rental car transforms the trip.

Afternoon
Jusangjeolli Lava Cliffs Kakao Maps
One of Jeju's most dramatic natural sights: perfect hexagonal basalt columns formed by Hallasan's lava meeting the sea — like the Giant's Causeway, but Korean. Right on the south coast. The viewing boardwalk takes 20–30 minutes and the light is excellent in afternoon. Free.
Late Afternoon
Cheonjiyeon Waterfall + Oedolgae Rock
Cheonjiyeon Waterfall — three-tiered waterfall cascading through subtropical forest into a turquoise pool. The boardwalk approach is beautiful. Said to have healing properties.

Oedolgae Rock — a dramatic 20-metre volcanic monolith rising alone from the sea, visible from a clifftop viewpoint with a short coastal trail. Featured in multiple K-dramas.
Evening
Jeju Black Pork BBQ
Black pigs (heukdwaeji) are raised on a diet that includes barley and fermented food, giving the pork a richer, more complex flavour than mainland pork. The restaurants here wrap your jacket in a plastic bag before the grilling starts — so it doesn't smell of smoke. This is the definitive Jeju meal. Pork belly and neck cuts at the table, grilled by the staff, wrapped in perilla leaves.
Best with Jeju hallabong soju (citrus-flavoured, lighter than mainland) or local Hallasan makgeolli. The black pork street near Jeju City airport area has dozens of competing restaurants — in Seogwipo, try Heukdwaeji Sikdang.

Complete Korean Food & Drink Guide ▼
Bindaetteok — Mung Bean Pancake
Where: Gwangjang Market, Seoul
Ground mung beans mixed with pork, bean sprouts, and kimchi, pan-fried until golden. Crispy outside, moist inside. The single best thing to eat at Gwangjang. Pair with makgeolli — this is the classic Korean combination. ₩5,000/pancake.
Mayak Gimbap — "Drug" Rice Rolls
Where: Gwangjang Market (Original Mayak Gimbap stall)
Half the size of regular kimbap, packed with sesame oil, seeds, and pickled radish. The name means "drug" — because you genuinely cannot stop eating them. Queue is always long but moves fast. Order at least 2 rolls. ₩3,000/roll.
Yukhoe — Korean Beef Tartare
Where: Gwangjang Market 2nd floor yukhoe alley
Fresh Hanwoo beef julienned by hand and tossed with sesame oil, pear, garlic, and egg yolk. The quality at Gwangjang is extraordinarily high — vendors source fresh beef every morning. Try the yukhoe + nakji (octopus) set (₩20,000). Pairs beautifully with makgeolli.
Samgyeopsal — Pork Belly BBQ
Where: Any Korean BBQ restaurant
Thick-cut pork belly grilled at the table. Wrap in perilla leaf or lettuce with sliced garlic, gochujang, and kimchi. The act of wrapping (ssam) is social and interactive. Order soju bombs (somaek) alongside. Budget ₩15,000–20,000/person for a full spread.
Galbi — Marinated Short Rib BBQ
Where: Upscale Korean BBQ restaurants
Beef short ribs marinated overnight in soy, garlic, pear, and sesame. More premium and refined than samgyeopsal. The caramelisation on the grill is extraordinary. Always served with banchan (unlimited side dishes). Try bone-in galbi for the real experience.
Dwaeji Gukbap — Pork Rice Soup
Where: Busan (Seomyeon, Haeundae area restaurants)
Milky pork bone broth simmered for hours, with sliced boiled pork and rice. Season with salted shrimp, chives, and gochujang from the condiment pots on the table. Busan's unofficial breakfast and hangover cure. ₩8,000–10,000/bowl.
Milmyeon — Cold Wheat Noodles
Where: Busan (Seomyeon, Choryang Milmyeon Street)
Busan's signature dish — chewy wheat noodles in cold tangy broth (mul milmyeon) or spicy sauce (bibim milmyeon). Made from flour provided by the US Army to Korean War refugees — genuinely historical food. Refreshing even in winter. ₩9,000–10,000/bowl.
Eomuk — Fish Cake Skewers
Where: Street stalls everywhere in Busan, Busan Station area
Busan is the fish cake capital of Korea. Spongy, savoury fish cake on skewers in a light broth. The broth (mul-tteok) is served free in cups on the side — drink it. Cheap (₩1,000/skewer) and comforting. Samjin Eomuk Cafe near Nampo-dong does an elevated version.
Heukdwaeji — Jeju Black Pork BBQ
Where: Jeju Island — Seogwipo and Jeju City black pork streets
Jeju's black pigs produce richer, more complex pork than mainland breeds. The flavour difference is real — richer marbling, deeper taste, chewier texture. Always grilled at the table, always with a plastic bag for your jacket, always with Jeju soju and perilla leaves. ₩15,000–20,000/person.
Jeonbok Juk — Abalone Porridge
Where: Jeju — Seogwipo Maeil Olle Market, coastal restaurants
Green-tinged rice porridge made with abalone and its digestive tract (which gives the green colour). Deeply oceanic, creamy, and intensely savoury. One of the most uniquely Jeju foods. Best as a morning meal after a hike or as recovery food after the beach.
Pajeon + Makgeolli
Where: Traditional restaurants, pojangmacha, mountain trailheads
Savoury green onion pancake (pajeon) with a cold bowl of makgeolli (milky fizzy rice wine). On a rainy day, this pairing is one of Korea's most beloved cultural rituals — even the name for rainy weather in Korean is associated with pajeon cravings. Absolutely do this at a mountain trailhead after hiking.
Chimaek — Fried Chicken + Beer
Where: Everywhere, BHC near Myeongdong especially celebrated
Korea's most beloved casual food culture. Double-fried Korean fried chicken (crispy, lighter batter than Western) served with beer. Eaten late at night while watching sports, after drinking, or just because. Order half soy-garlic, half spicy. BHC and Kyochon are the dominant national chains.

Korean Drinking Culture — The Full Guide ▼
Soju
Korea's national spirit — distilled rice wine, 16–25% ABV. Original soju (Jinro, Chamisul) has a sharp, clean taste. Fruit sojus (grapefruit, strawberry, peach) are sweeter and very popular with tourists. Served cold in small shot glasses. Always pour for others, not yourself.
Makgeolli
Unfiltered, milky, fizzy rice wine. 5–8% ABV. Slightly sweet and tangy, with a lightly fizzy quality. Traditionally served in a large bowl or kettle. The ideal pairing is pajeon (pancake) — especially on a rainy day. Best tried at Gwangjang Market or a traditional makgeolli bar.
Somaek
The beloved Korean "bomb" — pour a shot of soju into half a glass of beer. Classic ratio is 3:7 (soju to beer). Stir by spinning the shot glass with two chopsticks. This is how most Koreans actually drink at dinner.
Anju
Drinking food — essential in Korean drinking culture. You always eat while drinking. Anju can be anything from dried squid and peanuts to pajeon, tteokbokki, or fried chicken. Ordering food with alcohol is expected at pojangmacha and bars.
Pouring etiquette
Pour drinks for everyone at the table before filling your own. Use two hands (or right hand supported at the wrist) when pouring and receiving. Say "geonbae!" for cheers. Never let someone's glass stay empty.
Pojangmacha
Canvas tent pubs — fold-up tables on the street or in covered alley spaces, serving cheap soju, beer, makgeolli, and anju. An institution. Best experienced late at night when the city is winding down. Find them near universities and entertainment districts.
Craft Beer
Best spots: Seongsu (Amazing Brewing Co., Seoul Brewery), Euljiro (Euljiro Brewing, Artmonster), Itaewon (Magpie, The Booth), Hongdae (Magpie Brewing branch, Seoul Brewery Hapjeong). Korean craft beer has become genuinely excellent — IPAs, sours, and experimental brews.
Jjimjilbang
Korean communal sauna — a genuine cultural experience, not just a spa. Communal areas (pyjamas provided) include themed sauna rooms, sleeping areas, and food courts. Women's/men's bathing areas are separate and clothing-free. Dragon Hill Spa (Yongsan) and Heosimcheong (Busan) are the best. ₩10,000–15,000 all-in.
Cultural Etiquette ▼
Bowing
A slight bow (15°) is the standard greeting. Deep bows are for formal occasions. Nodding while saying annyeonghaseyo (안녕하세요) is perfectly appropriate for most interactions.
Two hands
Use two hands (or right hand + left supporting the wrist) when giving or receiving anything: money, cards, gifts, food, business cards. This shows respect, especially to elders.
Shoes
Remove shoes when entering homes and some traditional restaurants. A shoe rack at the entrance is the signal. Never step on the raised floor area with shoes on.
Eating together
Korean meals are communal — dishes are shared from the centre. Never stick chopsticks vertically into rice (funeral association). Elders eat first. Don't refuse food that's been offered.
Photography
Always ask before photographing individuals, especially ajummas (older women) at markets. Koreans have constitutionally protected portrait rights — photographing someone without consent can be legally problematic.
Useful phrases
Annyeonghaseyo — hello | Gamsahamnida — thank you | Juseyo — please give me [this] | Eolmayeyo? — how much? | Geonbae! — cheers! | Jugeunde? — is [this] available? Koreans respond warmly to any attempt.

The original milmyeon institution in Busan. Near Busan Station. One of the three legendary milmyeon establishments — the granddaddy of the dish. 225 Jungang-daero, Choryang-dong, Dong-gu. Open 10am–10pm. Small, old, atmospheric. The bowl tastes of postwar Busan history.

The definitive Jeju black pork dinner — in Seogwipo near your hotel. Staff wrap your jacket in a plastic bag before service starts. Jeju's signature hairtail fish (galchi) — only found here. Hairtail braised in a rich, slightly spicy soy-gochujang sauce with radish and vegetables. Dombedon Heukdwaeji Map
Another outstanding black pork option — different style. Serves dombedon — a Jeju-style thick-cut boiled black pork presented cold on a wooden board, with ssamjang and garlic. Haenyeo's fresh catch, Seongsan Map
Raw seafood directly from haenyeo divers — at the base of Seongsan Ilchulbong. After descending from Sunrise Peak, the female free-divers sell their catch from small stalls by the shore between 9–11am: abalone, sea urchin, conch. Sit on a plastic stool, eat raw seafood with soju, look out at the sea. The most unique breakfast or early lunch in Korea.

🥃 Korean Drinks — The Full Masterclass ▼
Soju — the basics
Korea's national spirit, 16–25% ABV. Original Chamisul (참이슬) and Jinro (진로) are the classic clear rice sojus — clean, slightly sweet, sharp finish. Fruit sojus (grapefruit, strawberry, peach, lychee) are sweeter and 13–14% — excellent entry point. Always served cold in small glasses. Never pour your own — pour for others first.
Makgeolli
Unfiltered milky rice wine, 5–8% ABV. Slightly fizzy, mildly sweet and tangy. Served in metal bowls or a large kettle (often you shake the bottle first). The traditional pairing is pajeon (pancake) — ideally at a mountain trailhead after hiking. Best tried at Sanullim 1992 (Seoul) or Gwangjang Market. Try premium artisanal versions at Namsan Sool Club.
Somaek
The Korean bomb drink — the way most Koreans actually drink at dinner. Pour a shot of soju into half a glass of beer. Classic ratio: 3:7. Stir by spinning the shot glass with two chopsticks (the "tornado" method). Tastes cleaner and lighter than either drink alone. Order at any Korean BBQ restaurant — it's the default dinner drink.
Hallabong Soju
Jeju-specific citrus soju — lighter and more aromatic than mainland soju. Made with Jeju's famous hallabong tangerine. The defining Jeju drink — order it automatically with black pork BBQ. Also available in Jeju tangerine makgeolli, which is excellent.
Korean craft beer
The scene has exploded in quality. Best breweries: Magpie (sour specialists — try The Ghost), Euljiro Brewing (IPAs and Saisons), Artmonster (peanut stout is extraordinary), Seoul Brewery (Rye IPA, Choco Loco Porter), Amazing Brewing Co (experimental, Seongsu flagship). All available in taprooms across Seoul. ₩7,500–12,000/pint.
Sikhye
Sweet, cloudy fermented rice punch — the non-alcoholic essential. Served cold at jjimjilbang and traditional restaurants. Made from malted barley and rice with a slightly sweet, clean flavour. The classic jjimjilbang food pairing is sikhye + boiled egg. A genuine cultural institution.
Korean coffee culture
World-class specialty coffee across Seoul. Top roasters and cafés: Anthracite (Hongdae/Hapjeong — industrial-chic, outstanding single-origin), Fritz Coffee (Mapo — pastel, precise, exceptional), Felt Coffee (Itaewon — minimal, careful). These are the cafés that compete internationally. Seongsu has a dozen equally excellent new-wave coffee shops. Budget ₩6,000–8,000 for a specialty pour.
Maekju culture
Mass-market Korean lager — Hite, Cass, Terra, Kloud. Cheap (₩3,000–5,000), served everywhere. The standard pairing with chimaek (fried chicken) and dried snacks at a pojangmacha. Terra is generally considered the best of the mainstream lagers. Kloud uses 100% malt and is slightly richer. Upgrade to craft when you can, but know the local lagers too.

📋 Practical 2026 Updates — Read Before You Go ▼
Seoul Transit Pass (NEW)
Seoul Climate Card Tourist Pass — launched 2025, major upgrade from T-money. Unlimited rides on Seoul subway + city buses for ₩5,000/day (1-day), ₩8,000 (2-day), ₩10,000 (3-day), ₩15,000 (5-day), ₩20,000 (7-day), plus ₩3,000 card fee. As of March 2026, you can buy and top up using international Visa, Mastercard, Amex, and UnionPay at 440 Seoul Metro kiosks. Far better value than paying per ride. Note: Cannot be used at Incheon Airport — buy a standard T-money card for the AREX airport train, then switch to Climate Card in Seoul.
K-ETA Waiver 2026
K-ETA requirement waived until December 31, 2026 for 22 countries. If you hold a passport from Australia, Austria, Belgium, Canada, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Hong Kong, Italy, Japan, Macao, Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, Poland, Singapore, Spain, Sweden, Taiwan, UK, or US — you do NOT need a K-ETA for this trip. From January 1, 2027 it becomes mandatory again. No action required for 2026 — just bring your passport.
Maps + Navigation
Google Maps does not work for navigation in South Korea — due to national security restrictions, it cannot provide walking or transit directions. Download Naver Map (recommended — most accurate, works in English) and KakaoMap before you land. Both require data to work, so set up your eSIM or SIM before arrival. If a restaurant search fails in English, Google the name, copy the Korean characters, and paste into Naver — works perfectly.
eSIM vs Physical SIM
eSIM is the best option for most travelers. Buy and activate before departure from Airalo, Holafly, or KT (official). No queuing at the airport. Data-only plans are fine — use WhatsApp/KakaoTalk for calls. Physical SIMs require in-person passport registration at a Korean telecom shop. If you need a Korean number (for certain app verifications), get a Data + Voice plan. For most people: a 10-day 5–10GB data eSIM is enough for maps, messaging, and social media across Seoul, Busan, and Jeju.
Essential Apps
Install before landing: Naver Map (navigation), KakaoTalk (messaging, most locals use it), KakaoT (taxi hailing — works like Uber, often cheaper and always honest metering), Papago (superior Korean translator, by Naver), Korail app (train booking). Optional: Coupang Eats (food delivery if self-catering), Naver Blog (Korean restaurant reviews — better than TripAdvisor locally). For hikes: Naver Hiking Map (offline trail data).
Cash vs Card
South Korea is mostly card-friendly but carry some cash (₩50,000–100,000) for street food, pojangmacha tents, some traditional markets, and taxi tips. ATMs: use machines marked "Global ATM" or with Visa/Mastercard logos — GS25 and 7-Eleven ATMs reliably accept foreign cards. Always select "charge in local currency" when asked, to avoid dynamic currency conversion fees. Tax refund: shop at stores with a "Tax Free" sign and bring your passport — you can claim VAT back at the airport on departure for purchases over ₩30,000.

🎵 Live Music, Jazz & Indie Culture ▼
🎷 Seoul Jazz Festival
May 22–24 📅
One of Asia's best jazz festivals — and you'll be in Seoul for it. 60+ artists across multiple outdoor stages at Olympic Park, blending international headliners with Korean jazz and contemporary artists. Free entry at the gate; beer and food tokens purchased inside. 2025 drew 50,000 attendees. Olympic Park: Line 5 → Mongchontoseong, 20 min from Hongdae. Buy tickets in advance at seonjazz.co.kr — weekend day passes sell out. This is a don't-miss while you're based in Hongdae.
Hongdae — Indie & Rock Rolling Hall · Strange Fruit · Club FF · Jandari St
Rolling Hall — 20+ years of Hongdae indie. The most important mid-sized venue; weeknights feature new bands, weekends bring established acts. Entry ₩20,000–30,000. Check Instagram for the schedule.
Strange Fruit — basement indie institution since 2004. Blues, rock, alt. Almost nightly live sets. Very local crowd, zero tourists.
Club FF — the best alternative/indie club. Rock, punk, ska, metal. All-you-can-drink midnight hour. Grungy basement, small floor, big energy. Entry ~₩20,000.
Jandari Street — a narrow alley threading through Hongdae packed with live music bars. Home of the annual Zandari Festa (Korea's SXSW equivalent, usually October). Walk it any Friday or Saturday night.
Jazz — Itaewon / Gyeongridan Boogie Woogie · All That Jazz
Boogie Woogie (Gyeongridan-gil, near Itaewon) — Seoul's best intimate jazz and soul bar. Live every night: jazz, funk, blues, soul, with a rotating lineup. No entry fee but ₩5,000–10,000 donation expected for the band. Small, atmospheric, and beloved — book a table for Friday/Saturday. Pet Sounds bar is directly upstairs for indie rock after jazz.
All That Jazz — Seoul's legendary jazz venue since 1976. Entrance ₩30,000pp includes food. Live bands nightly; note weekend shows sometimes lean into blues and R&B.
Hidden Scene Gopchang Jeongol
Gopchang Jeongol (Hongdae) — "Korean Traditional Pops Club since 2001." Walls covered in Korean rock and psychedelic records from the 1960s–90s. The owner is a walking encyclopedia of Korean underground music history. DJ sets and occasional live shows. Very local — no tourists, strong sense of place. One of the most culturally specific bars in Seoul.
Finding shows
Most Korean venues post schedules on Instagram 3–5 days ahead — follow venues you're interested in. DoIndie.com lists upcoming Seoul indie shows in English. For jazz: seonjazz.co.kr. Most Hongdae venues accept walk-ins; mid-week shows rarely sell out, but Friday/Saturday at popular spots — book through their Instagram or Naver reservation link. Live shows typically start 8pm; clubs peak after 11pm.

🍺 Gwangalli Craft Beer Crawl
The best single craft beer evening in Busan — all within a 5-min walk of each other along Gwangalli Beach. Start at Gorilla Brewing (open windows facing the bridge, 12 house taps — Tiramisu Stout and Newing IPA are standouts, beach views). Move to Wild Wave Brew / Sour Yeongdo (specialty sour ales, Surleim Dry-Hopped Sour is the signature — also does wine and experimental collabs). Then Galmegi Brewing Gwangan (Busan's original craft brewery, English-style ales, chill atmosphere, soju barrel-aged imperial stout if available). End at the beach with a convenience store beer watching the Gwangan Bridge light show. Budget ₩40,000–60,000pp for a proper crawl with snacks.
